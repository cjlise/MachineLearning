Employees turnover study using survival analysis

The goal of this project is to carry out the analysis of employee churn. We will work with a dataset available on github. The dataset description is also available on Kaggle, althouth it cannot be freely downloaded from there.

Here is the dataset description: it‚Äôs a csv file with 15 000 rows (1 row for column headers and 14 499 rows for employees information) and 10 columns.

Here are the columns description:

  • satisfaction_level - Employee satisfaction level
  • last_evaluation - Last evaluation score
  • number_project - Number of projects assigned to
  • average_monthly_hour - Average monthly hours worked
  • time_spend_company - Time spent at the company. Between 2 and 10.
  • Work_accident - (1= Yes, 0 = No) - Whether they have had a work accident
  • left - Whether or not employee left company (1 = Yes, 0 = No). This is the censoring information.
  • promotion_last_5year - (1 = Yes, 0 = No) - Whether they have had a promotion in the last 5 years
  • sales - Department name (not just sales)
  • salary - Salary category

Kaplan-Meier estimator

As we have a time variable (time_spend_company) and a censoring variable (left), we can use Kaplan-Meier estimator to carry out the survival analysis of the employees turnover. We draw below the Kaplan-Meier survival curve using the R Survival package.

library(survival)
St <- survfit(Surv(time_spend_company,left) ~ 1, data = h)
St
## Call: survfit(formula = Surv(time_spend_company, left) ~ 1, data = h)
## 
##       n  events  median 0.95LCL 0.95UCL 
##   14999    3571       6       5       6
plot(St, main="Kaplan-Meier estimator", xlab="Time", ylab="Survival probability")

The median value returned by the survfit function is 6 years. Therefore the average lifetime at the company for an employee is 6 years. The confidence interval for the median is (5,6). We also notice that the survival probability is constant after 6 years.

Comparing groups

We will compare the groups for categorical varaiables using the log rank test: Work_accident, promotion_last_5years, number_project, department, and salary.

test_variable <- function(var_name) {
  h$x <- h[[ var_name ]]
  survdiff(Surv(time_spend_company,left) ~ x, data = h)
}

logrank_tests <-
  tibble(variable = c("Work_accident", "prom_l5years","number_project", "dept", "salary")) %>%
  mutate(obj = map(variable, test_variable),
         tab = map(obj, broom::glance)) %>%
  unnest(tab)
logrank_tests

Cox Hazard Ratio Analysis:
Most of the hazard ratio coefficients are significants (P-values very small or less than 5%) except for some of the department levels.

The variable with the more higher coefficient is number_project3. And this coefficient is negative and therefore reduce the risk: Working on 3 projects reduce the risk by a factor 49 CI(36, 66) compared to working on 2 projects (which is the reference level). And we see also that working on 4 projects decrease the risk by a factor 11.9 CI( 10.3, 13.7)compared to working on 2 projects. And the risk is decreased by a factor 7 CI(6.2,8), 4.9 CI(4.2, 5.6) and 3.2 CI(2.6, 3.9) for respectively 5, 6 and 7 projects.

The next most higher coefficient is the satisfaction level: An increase by one unit (which is the full range) reduces the risk by a factor 3.95 CI(3.3, 4.8).

Then in the coefficient range we have the salary impact: risk is increased by a factor 3.38 CI(2.6, 4.4) from Salary_high to Salary_low, and by a factor 2.4 CI(1.8, 3.2) from salary_high to salary_medium.

Then we have in terms of importance level: prom_l5yearsyes. A promotion in the last 5 years reduce the risk by a factor 3.5 CI (2,6.3) compared to no promotion.

Next coefficient is Work_accidentyes: The risk is reduced by a factor 2.9 CI( 2.4,3.5) compared to Work_accidentNo. This is counter intuitive, but looking at the exploratory plot, the proportion of Work_accidentyes is lower for people who left.

There is not much difference in hazard ratio coefficient for the department variable. The higher evidence, is that risk for R&D departent compared to accounting is lower by a factor 1.6 CI(1.2, 2.2).

Conclusion

We analyzed the employees turnover of a company using survival analysis modeling over a period of 10 time unit that we assumed to be years. After an exploratory analysis, we computed the Kaplan-Meier estimator for survival probability. Then we identified that the median survival at the company is 6 years: the expected time spent at the company is 6 years. Then we used the log rank test to check if the levels in the 5 categorical features have an impact on the survival. The p-value for the log rank test is zero for all the categorical variables. Therefore for all the categorical variables we reject the null hypothesis that there is no difference between the categories defined by these variables.

We then splitted the initial 14499 rows dataset into one training dataset of 10000 rows and a testing dataset of 4999 rows, before to carry out the modeling part.

We first ran a Cox regression on the training set and identified hazard ratio coefficients with the most impact on the risk: the number of projects. And specifically the coefficient for 3 projects which has a factor of 44 compared to the reference level of 2 projects. This coefficient is negative, and therefore this coefficient is for reduction of the risk.

We then carried out model selection using AIC and GLMNET. AIC returned the full model as the best model and GLMNET returned a model with 14 coefficients out of 21.

We then did some predictions using the test dataset and use cox regression again to assess the accuracy of the models. Both model gave a very high concordance index of 0.913 and AUC at 4 years of 0.82.
